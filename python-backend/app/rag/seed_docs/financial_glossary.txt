FINANCIAL ANALYSIS GLOSSARY — KEY TERMS FOR EQUITY VALUATION

This document defines core financial metrics used in fundamental analysis, DCF modelling, and equity valuation. MarketCoach uses these definitions when interpreting company financials and constructing investment theses.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VALUATION MULTIPLES
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PRICE-TO-EARNINGS RATIO (P/E)
Definition: Market price per share ÷ Earnings per share (EPS).
Interpretation:
  — A high P/E (>30×) signals market expectation of high future growth, or premium quality business.
  — A low P/E (<15×) may indicate value opportunity or declining growth prospects.
  — P/E must be compared within the same industry — tech companies (25-40×) vs banks (10-15×).
  — Trailing P/E uses last 12 months actual earnings; Forward P/E uses next 12 months consensus estimates.
  — Cyclical businesses (steel, semiconductors) may show misleading P/E at earnings peaks or troughs.
Typical ranges by sector: Technology 25-40×, Consumer Staples 20-25×, Energy 10-15×, Utilities 12-18×, Banks 10-14×.

PRICE-TO-BOOK RATIO (P/B)
Definition: Market price per share ÷ Book value per share (shareholders' equity / shares outstanding).
Interpretation:
  — P/B < 1.0 means market values the company below its accounting net assets — often signals distress or value trap.
  — P/B > 5× is common for asset-light businesses (software, pharma) with high intangible value not captured on balance sheet.
  — Most useful for financial sector stocks (banks, insurance).
  — Warren Buffett historically used P/B < 1.2× as a buy signal.

EV/EBITDA (Enterprise Value to EBITDA)
Definition: Enterprise Value ÷ EBITDA (Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, Amortisation).
  Enterprise Value = Market Cap + Total Debt − Cash and Cash Equivalents.
  EBITDA = Operating Income + D&A.
Interpretation:
  — Capital structure-neutral metric (accounts for debt). Preferred for comparing leveraged companies.
  — EV/EBITDA < 10× generally considered inexpensive; > 20× signals premium growth expectation.
  — Widely used in M&A: private equity typically targets companies at 8-12× EBITDA.
  — Limitation: does not account for differences in capital intensity (high CapEx companies appear cheaper than they are).
Typical ranges: Software/SaaS 20-40×, Consumer brands 12-20×, Industrials 8-12×, Telecoms 6-9×.

PRICE-TO-SALES RATIO (P/S)
Definition: Market capitalisation ÷ Annual revenue.
Interpretation:
  — Useful for pre-earnings or high-growth companies where P/E is not meaningful.
  — Technology companies: 5-15× is common; >20× signals extreme growth expectations.
  — P/S > 20× requires sustained 30%+ annual revenue growth to be justified.
  — During speculative bubbles, P/S can reach 30-50× before mean reverting dramatically.

PEG RATIO
Definition: P/E ratio ÷ Earnings growth rate (5-year expected CAGR).
Interpretation:
  — PEG < 1.0: potentially undervalued relative to growth (Peter Lynch's rule of thumb).
  — PEG 1.0-2.0: fairly valued.
  — PEG > 2.0: expensive relative to growth.
  — Limitation: highly sensitive to growth rate assumptions; small changes in expected growth drastically alter PEG.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
DISCOUNTED CASH FLOW (DCF) ANALYSIS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DCF FAIR VALUE
Definition: The intrinsic value of a stock calculated by discounting projected future free cash flows to present value.

Formula:
  Fair Value = Σ [FCF_t / (1 + WACC)^t] + Terminal Value / (1 + WACC)^n

Where:
  FCF_t = Free Cash Flow in year t (typically projected 5-10 years)
  WACC = Weighted Average Cost of Capital (discount rate)
  Terminal Value = FCF_n × (1 + g) / (WACC − g), where g = long-term growth rate

WACC COMPONENTS:
  — Cost of Equity (CAPM): Risk-Free Rate + Beta × Equity Risk Premium
    Example: 4.5% + 1.2 × 5.5% = 11.1% cost of equity
  — Cost of Debt: Effective interest rate × (1 − Tax Rate) (after-tax, since interest is tax-deductible)
  — WACC = (Equity / Total Capital) × Cost of Equity + (Debt / Total Capital) × Cost of Debt

MARGIN OF SAFETY
Definition: (DCF Fair Value − Current Market Price) / DCF Fair Value × 100.
Interpretation:
  — Positive margin of safety: stock trades below intrinsic value.
  — Negative margin of safety: stock trades above intrinsic value (overvalued by DCF).
  — Benjamin Graham recommended requiring at least 33% margin of safety before buying.
  — Value investors typically require 30-50% margin of safety to account for DCF model uncertainty.

FREE CASH FLOW (FCF)
Definition: Operating Cash Flow − Capital Expenditure (CapEx).
Significance:
  — FCF is "real" cash available to shareholders after maintaining and growing the business.
  — FCF > Net Income: positive sign — earnings quality is high (non-cash charges are real).
  — FCF < Net Income: negative sign — aggressive accruals, heavy CapEx, or working capital build.
  — FCF Yield = FCF / Market Cap. FCF Yield > 5% is generally considered attractive.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
PROFITABILITY & EFFICIENCY METRICS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

GROSS MARGIN
Definition: (Revenue − Cost of Goods Sold) / Revenue × 100.
Interpretation:
  — Software/SaaS: 70-85% gross margin is typical.
  — Consumer electronics: 30-45%.
  — Automotive: 15-25%.
  — Commodity businesses: 5-15%.
  — Expanding gross margin over time is a bullish signal (pricing power, operating leverage).

OPERATING MARGIN (EBIT Margin)
Definition: Operating Income / Revenue × 100.
  Operating Income = Gross Profit − Operating Expenses (SG&A, R&D).
Interpretation:
  — Operating margin shows core business profitability before financing costs and taxes.
  — Best-in-class: Apple 30%, Microsoft 45%, Nvidia 55%.
  — Companies with improving operating margins on rising revenue demonstrate operating leverage.

NET PROFIT MARGIN
Definition: Net Income / Revenue × 100.
Interpretation:
  — Influenced by interest expense, tax rate, and non-operating items.
  — Less reliable for cross-company comparison than operating margin.

RETURN ON EQUITY (ROE)
Definition: Net Income / Average Shareholders' Equity × 100.
Interpretation:
  — ROE > 20% is considered excellent and indicates a strong competitive moat.
  — Warren Buffett looks for ROE > 15% sustained over 10+ years.
  — Caveat: High ROE can be artificially inflated by high financial leverage (debt).
  — Use DuPont decomposition: ROE = Net Margin × Asset Turnover × Equity Multiplier.

RETURN ON INVESTED CAPITAL (ROIC)
Definition: NOPAT (Net Operating Profit After Tax) / Invested Capital × 100.
  Invested Capital = Total Debt + Shareholders' Equity − Cash.
Interpretation:
  — ROIC > WACC creates shareholder value (the company earns more than its cost of capital).
  — ROIC < WACC destroys value — a warning signal even if earnings are growing.
  — Sustained ROIC > 20% is a hallmark of wide-moat businesses (Apple: ~58%, Microsoft: ~35%).

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
DEBT & LIQUIDITY METRICS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DEBT-TO-EQUITY RATIO (D/E)
Definition: Total Debt / Shareholders' Equity.
Interpretation:
  — D/E < 0.5: conservative, low financial risk.
  — D/E 0.5-2.0: moderate — typical for industrial and consumer companies.
  — D/E > 3.0: highly leveraged — common in telecoms, utilities, REITs, and private equity-backed companies.
  — Negative equity (buybacks exceeding retained earnings) can produce meaningless or negative D/E (e.g. Apple, McDonald's).

NET DEBT
Definition: Total Debt (short-term + long-term) − Cash and Cash Equivalents.
Interpretation:
  — Negative net debt = "net cash" position — company has more cash than debt.
  — Net debt/EBITDA < 2× is generally considered safe. > 4× signals financial stress.

INTEREST COVERAGE RATIO
Definition: EBIT / Interest Expense.
Interpretation:
  — > 5×: comfortable coverage.
  — 2-3×: adequate but leaves limited buffer.
  — < 1.5×: danger zone — earnings may not cover interest obligations under stress.

CURRENT RATIO
Definition: Current Assets / Current Liabilities.
Interpretation:
  — > 2.0: strong short-term liquidity.
  — 1.0-2.0: adequate.
  — < 1.0: potential short-term liquidity risk.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
GROWTH METRICS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

REVENUE GROWTH (CAGR)
Definition: (Ending Revenue / Beginning Revenue)^(1/n) − 1, where n = number of years.
Interpretation:
  — Hyper-growth (>40% CAGR): Rare, typically early-stage SaaS or AI companies.
  — High growth (20-40%): Premium valuation justified.
  — Moderate growth (10-20%): Market-average to above-average, standard P/E premium.
  — Low/no growth (<5%): Value stock territory unless exceptional capital return story.

EPS GROWTH
Definition: Year-over-year change in earnings per share.
Significance:
  — EPS growth driven by buybacks (share count reduction) is "lower quality" than organic earnings growth.
  — Sustainable EPS growth requires revenue growth or margin expansion, not just buybacks.

SECTOR-SPECIFIC CONTEXT
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

TECHNOLOGY / SaaS
Key metrics: ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue), NRR (Net Revenue Retention), LTV/CAC ratio, Rule of 40 (Revenue Growth % + FCF Margin % > 40%).
Best-in-class NRR: >120% (customers expand usage over time, driving organic growth).
Rule of 40 benchmark: >40 indicates healthy balance of growth and profitability.

SEMICONDUCTORS
Key metrics: Book-to-bill ratio (orders vs billings — >1.0 indicates growing demand), gross margin (45-65% for fabless design houses), CapEx intensity.
Cyclical warning signs: inventory build-up, book-to-bill < 0.9, customer order cancellations.

FINANCIAL SERVICES / BANKS
Key metrics: Net Interest Margin (NIM), Return on Assets (ROA), Return on Equity (ROE), Tier 1 Capital Ratio (regulatory capital buffer), Non-Performing Loan (NPL) ratio.
Healthy bank benchmarks: NIM >3%, ROE >12%, Tier 1 >10%, NPL <1%.

CONSUMER / RETAIL
Key metrics: Same-store sales growth (SSS), gross margin, inventory turnover, store count growth.
Red flags: declining SSS with store count growth (growth masking declining unit economics).

ENERGY
Key metrics: Break-even oil price, reserve replacement ratio, cash flow per barrel, EV/2P (enterprise value per proved and probable reserves).
